# Slides — Content

Density, hierarchy, and voice for presentation copy.

**See also:** [voice-and-tone.md](../../brand/voice-and-tone.md), [writing-style.md](../../content/writing-style.md).

---

## Density and hierarchy

- **One idea per slide.** The slide title is the headline; body copy supports it. If you need an "and also" you need another slide.
- **Title carries the argument**, not the topic. Prefer "Moving from on-premise monoliths to many digital services on paved roads" over "John Lewis case study".
- **Lead sentence is the elevator pitch.** Bold it. A reader skimming bolded leads should still get the argument.
- **Bullets are a fallback, not a default.** Prose paragraphs read more confidently. Reach for bullets when the content is genuinely a list (e.g., outcomes, services, steps).

---

## Density by delivery mode

How dense a slide should be depends on how it will be consumed:

- **Presented live** — a presenter carries the narrative. Slides are sparse: headline, a supporting visual, minimal text. The deck does not stand alone.
- **Consumed without a presenter** — read-ahead, left-behind, or forwarded to someone who wasn't in the room. Each slide must make its point cold, with no verbal explanation.

**EE default: assume "consumed without a presenter".** Most EE decks are left behind or forwarded, so default to **self-contained but lean** — every slide stands alone if forwarded, yet nothing becomes a wall of text. The balance:

- The **headline states the takeaway** (the rule above) so a forwarded slide communicates its point in one line.
- Body copy is the minimum that makes the headline defensible without a speaker — no more.
- **Push depth into the notes pane**, not onto the slide: it preserves detail for the reader who wants it while keeping the slide scannable.
- Tests: if a slide only makes sense with someone talking over it, it's too sparse for a left-behind deck; if it reads like a document page, it's too dense. Go sparser **only** when the deck is genuinely presented live and won't be forwarded.

---

## Voice

- Plain English, active voice, present tense.
- Refer to EE as "we"; refer to the client by name on case-study slides, "our client" when anonymised.
- Avoid hedges ("we believe we can help you potentially achieve…") — make the claim or remove it.
- Avoid jargon stacking. "Cloud-native, AI-accelerated, event-driven platform" → pick the one that matters to this audience.

---

## Do

- Write the title last. Draft the body, then write the title that summarises the argument.
- Read each slide aloud — if you stumble, the sentence is too long.

---

## Don't

- Do not include speaker notes inside the slide body. Notes go in the notes pane.
- Do not pad to fill space. Empty area is fine.
- Do not include URLs in body copy unless they are the point of the slide; put them in the contact slide or notes.
